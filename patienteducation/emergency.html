Tooth Knocked Out (Avulsed Tooth):

When a tooth is knocked out, it's considered a dental emergency, and acting quickly is crucial. If this happens to you or someone else, try to handle the tooth carefully by picking it up by the crown (the top part) and avoiding touching the root. If the tooth is dirty, gently rinse it with milk or saline solution but refrain from scrubbing it. If possible, reinsert the tooth into its socket, making sure it's facing the right way. If reinsertion isn't feasible, store the tooth in a container with milk or a tooth preservation product to keep it moist. Immediate contact with your dentist or an emergency dentist is essential for the best chance of saving the tooth.



Severe Toothache:

A severe toothache can be excruciating and often indicates an underlying dental issue such as tooth decay, an abscess, or a cracked tooth. To alleviate the pain temporarily, rinse your mouth with warm water to clean it and gently floss to remove any trapped debris or food particles. Avoid placing painkillers directly on the affected area, as it can harm the gum tissue. Instead, contact your dentist promptly to schedule an appointment for an evaluation and appropriate treatment.

Broken or Fractured Tooth:

When a tooth breaks or fractures due to trauma or biting on a hard object, it can be a dental emergency. Begin by rinsing your mouth with warm water to clean it, and if there's bleeding, apply gentle pressure with gauze to stop it. If you can retrieve any broken tooth fragments, do so and keep them. To prevent injury to your mouth, cover any sharp edges with dental wax or sugarless gum. Then, contact your dentist immediately to arrange an appointment. Treatment options, including bonding, dental crowns, or extraction, will be determined by the dentist based on the extent of the fracture.
